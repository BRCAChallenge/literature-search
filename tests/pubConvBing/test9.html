Association between BRCA1 and BRCA2 mutations and cancer phenotype in Spanish breast/ovarian cancer families: Implications for genetic testing

Abstract

Index cases from a clinically relevant cohort of 102 Spanish families with at least 3 cases of breast and/or ovarian cancer (at least 1 case diagnosed before age 50) in the same lineage were screened for germline mutations in the entire coding sequence and intron boundaries of the breast cancer susceptibility genes BRCA1 and BRCA2. Overall, the prevalence of mutations was 43% in female breast/ovarian cancer families, 15% in female breast cancer families and 100% in male breast cancer families. Three recurrent mutations (185delAG, 589delCT and A1708E) explained 63% of BRCA1-related families. Early age at diagnosis of breast cancer, ovarian cancer, bilateral breast cancer, concomitant breast/ovarian cancer in a single patient and prostate cancer but not unilateral breast cancer were associated with BRCA1 and BRCA2 mutations. Male breast cancer was associated with BRCA2 mutations. The presence of male breast cancer was the only cancer phenotype that distinguished BRCA2- from BRCA1-related families. We have developed a logistic regression model for predicting the probability of harbouring a mutation in either BRCA1 or BRCA2 as a function of the cancer phenotype present in the family. The predictive positive and negative values of this model were 77.4% and 79%, respectively (probability cutoff of 30%). The findings of our work may be a useful tool for increasing the cost-effectiveness of genetic testing in familial cancer clinics. © 2001 Wiley-Liss, Inc.

Cancer of the breast is the most common malignancy among women in Western countries. In 5–10% of these patients, the disease can be attributed to an inherited predisposition.1 A linkage-based study estimated that the breast and ovarian cancer susceptibility genes BRCA1 and BRCA2 account, respectively, for 52% and 32% of all hereditary breast and/or ovarian cancer families with at least 4 breast cancer cases.2–4 Mutation-based studies indicated that defects in the 2 breast/ovarian cancer susceptibility genes account for a varying fraction of breast cancer families in different populations.5 The risk of breast and ovarian cancer associated with mutations in these 2 genes has been estimated,4 and other cancer types to which mutation carriers are predisposed have been identified.6–9

Today, many families with a moderate number of breast and/or ovarian cancer cases seek genetic testing in familial cancer clinics. Unfortunately, current laboratory methods are costly and time-consuming due to the fact that BRCA1 and BRCA2 mutations are apparently randomly distributed over the large coding sequence of both genes.10 A negative result does not indicate the absence of genetic predisposition in the family because PCR-based protocols do not detect genomic rearrangements and allele-specific transcription defects. Moreover, there is also evidence of other as yet unknown predisposing genes.4 Management of these families does not defer either without testing or with a negative testing result. Ideally, one would prefer to target the available resources for DNA testing to individuals with the highest probability of being a gene carrier. It is, therefore, important to find the clinical factors that could predict the presence of BRCA1 and BRCA2 mutations. This is best done by characterising the complete spectrum of mutations in a sufficient number of phenotypically fully characterised families. Probabilistic models of this kind are limited and restricted either to BRCA1 alone,11, 12 to the Ashkenazi Jewish population13 or to certain mutation mechanisms.14 Recently, a study involving both BRCA1 and BRCA2 genes in a series of Finish families with at least 3 cases of breast and/or ovarian cancer has been published.15 Although a direct comparison of these models is not possible due to different entry criteria, target populations, mutation screening protocols and clinical parameters analyzed, different predictive values for some clinical parameters such as bilateral breast cancer, breast and ovarian cancer in a single woman or unilateral breast cancer are suggested. It is conceivable that different probabilistic models must be developed for different target populations because the genetic background, environmental factors or specific prevalent BRCA1 or BRCA2 mutations may modify the outcome of the study.

The prevalence of BRCA1 and BRCA2 mutations among Spanish breast cancer families and their clinical features have not yet been thoroughly studied.16, 17 Here we report the study of BRCA1 and BRCA2 mutations in 102 Spanish families with at least 3 affected members, and we develop a logistic regression model predicting the probability of a family carrying a mutation as a function of the associated cancer phenotype.

Families

The cohort studied consisted of 102 families with 3 or more first- or second-degree relatives affected with breast and/or ovarian cancer in the same lineage with at least 1 case diagnosed before age 50. The Molecular Oncology Laboratory at San Carlos Hospital (Madrid) and the Department of Human Genetics, National Center of Cancer (Madrid) contributed 48 and 46 families (indexed respectively as SC or CNIO throughout this work), while the Institute Portuguese of Oncology (Porto) contributed 8 (5 Spanish and 3 Portuguese) families (indexed as IPO throughout this work). Some of these families have been included in previous works.16–18 Pedigrees were constructed on the basis of an index case considered to have the highest probability of being a deleterious mutation carrier (generally a woman with early-onset breast or ovarian cancer), with her first- to third-degree relatives on both parental sides being taken into consideration. All information concerning the family history of cancers, ages at onset, either ages at death or current ages and other clinical parameters were obtained by personal interview of the index case and most relatives. The data were verified on the basis of medical and pathologic records for all selected patients. After appropriate written consent was obtained, a blood sample was collected from each individual who decided to participate in our study.

In the total set of 102 families, 62 families showed breast cancer patients with on average 3.8 ± 1.25 cases per family. In one family, only ovarian cancer was diagnosed (CNIO 102). Thirty-nine families did include both types of cancer and had on average 3.2 ± 1.88 and 1.7 ±1 cases of breast and ovarian cancer, respectively. The mean age at diagnosis of breast cancer was 48.9 ± 8.25. The mean age at diagnosis of ovarian cancer was 48.8 ± 8.5.

Mutation analysis

The complete coding sequence and exon-intron boundaries of BRCA1 and BRCA2 were analysed for germline mutations using a combination of SSCP and protein truncation tests in 46 CNIO families17 or a combination of DGGE18 and f-CSGE19 in 48 SC families and 8 IPO families. Sequencing was performed using an automated fluorescent-based cycle sequencer and Rhodamine Dye Terminator kit (Applied Biosystems, Foster City, CA). All protocols are available upon request.

Statistical analysis

SPSS 8.0 for Windows was used in all analysis. Association between specific familial cancer phenotypes and the presence of BRCA1 or BRCA2 germline mutations were studied by univariate and multivariate analysis. For univariate analysis, Mann-Whitney and Fisher's exact tests were used. Factors that were significant (p < 0.01) were entered in multivariate analysis. Logistic regression with backward selection was used to calculate the logarithm of odds of carrying a pathogenic mutation as a linear function of significant variables from the univariate analysis.

The prevalence of BRCA1 mutations was approximately 3 times the prevalence of BRCA2 mutations both in female breast/ovarian (30% vs. 13%; p < 0.05) and female site-specific breast cancer families (10% vs. 3%; p < 0.01).

Our cohort included 5 families with cases of male breast cancer (3 breast cancer families and 2 breast/ovarian cancer families). All 5 families (100%) carried a BRCA2 mutation. There was only 1 ovarian cancer family in our cohort (5 affected members). This family carried a BRCA1 mutation.

The lowest rate of mutation prevalence was found among the group of families with 3 female breast cancer cases. In this group, only 1 family of 24 (4%; 95% CI 13–24.9%) carried a BRCA1 mutation (CNIO 170). Interestingly, the average age at diagnosis of breast cancer for this family was 31 years, 20 years lower than the average age at diagnosis of breast cancer for this group of families (50 years).

Mutation spectrum

We found 10 BRCA1 frame-shifting mutations (52.6%), 5 missense mutations (26.3%, all of them clustered in the C-terminal transcriptional domain) and 4 splicing alterations. We did not find any nonsense mutation (Table II). Ten mutations mapped in the 5′ end of the gene and 9 mapped in the 3′ end. It is interesting to note that no single mutation mapped in the central exon 11, which encompasses 60% of the BRCA1 coding sequence and a similar proportion of described mutations.10 We identified 3 recurrent mutations (185delAG in 5 families, 589delCT in 3 families and A1708E in 4 families), which explained 12 BRCA1-related families (63%; 95% CI 36–81%). These 3 recurrent mutations have been described previously in other populations.10 The BRCA1 mutation 185delAG is associated with the same BRCA1 haplotype first described in Ashkenazi Jews.16, 20 A1708E is one of the very few BRCA1 missense mutations known to have a pathogenic role.21, 22 G1706E was found in 2 affected sisters from the same family, but it was not detected in 2 healthy relatives and in 200 control chromosomes. This alteration would be expected to be significant, since it results in the substitution of a small hydrophobic amino acid by a hydrophilic-charged amino acid, and it is localised in the C-terminal transcriptional domain.17, 21 For these reasons, although the status of the G1706E variant is still uncertain, we have considered this mutation to have a high probability of being associated with the cancer in the family.

We identified 11 BRCA2 frame-shifting mutations (84.6%) and 2 nonsense mutations (15.4%). We did not identify mutations affecting splice sites (Table III). Families with bilateral breast cancers, ovarian cancers, breast and ovarian cancers in a single woman and prostate cancers were overrepresented in BRCA1/2-mutated families. By contrast, female unilateral breast cancer families were 5 times more frequent in the nonmutated group of families. It is interesting that the presence of cancer types other than breast, ovarian and prostate were 2 times more frequent in the nonmutated group of families. The median age at diagnosis of breast cancer was 6 years younger in BRCA1/2-mutated vs. nonmutated families. All these differences were statistically significant. On the other hand, the mean age at diagnosis of ovarian cancer and the number of families with at least 5 female breast cancers were identical in both mutated and nonmutated families.

We also tested whether these cancer phenotypes were differentially expressed in BRCA1- vs. BRCA2-mutated families (data not shown). Families with breast and ovarian cancer in a single woman were overrepresented (31.6% vs. 15.4%) in BRCA1-mutated families, although the difference was not statistically significant. Interestingly, families with at least 1 ovarian cancer were equally represented in both groups (57% vs. 54%).

Predicting the probability of finding a mutation in the Spanish population

On the basis of these clinical associations, we developed a logistic regression model to predict the probability of detecting a mutation in BRCA1 or BRCA2 as a function of cancer phenotype. In univariate analysis, bilateral breast cancer (p = 0.002), ovarian cancer (p = 0.005), breast and ovarian cancer in a single woman (p = 0.004), male breast cancer (p = 0.008), prostate cancer (p = 0.014) and mean age at diagnosis of breast cancer in the family (p = 0.009) were associated with BRCA1/2 mutations. Unilateral female breast cancer was not significantly associated (p = 0.186). Variables that were significant at the p < 0.01 level were entered into the multivariable analysis. The predictor variables were tested as ordinal/continuous and categorical variables in several different models. The best-fitting model was obtained by defining a new categorical variable that includes bilateral breast cancer, breast and ovarian cancer in a single woman and male breast cancer. The aim of our study focused on Spanish breast/ovarian families was 2-fold: first, to define clinical criteria that may help to select which of these families must be referred for BRCA1 and BRCA2 genetic testing and, second, to study whether the prevalence of mutations and/or the spectrum of mutations in these genes may have implications for the design of a genetic testing protocol suited for these families.

We addressed all these questions by screening BRCA1 and BRCA2 mutation status in 102 Spanish breast and/or ovarian families and extensively studying the associated cancer phenotype. Most of these families did not have a very extensive history of cancer and are representative of families commonly seen at a genetic counselling clinic.

The prevalence of mutations that we have found in our families (30%) is within the range observed in mutation-based studies for other populations.12, 14, 23–27 The breast cancer linkage consortium (BCLC) estimates that 95% of breast/ovarian cancer families and 65% of female breast cancer families are related to BRCA1 or BRCA2.4 We detected mutations in, respectively, 60% (8/13) and 23% (7/30) of our breast/ovarian and female breast cancer families with BCLC entry criteria (at least 4 breast cancer cases). The BCLC estimated that mutation screening methods found mutations in only 63% (95% CI 51–77%) of linked families.4 According to that, we estimate that in our population the prevalence of mutations in breast/ovarian families is in agreement with linkage-based predictions. By contrast, the prevalence of mutations in female breast cancer families seems to be lower. Further studies are needed to determine whether some mutations not detected with our current screening method (large genomic rearrangements or deletions) or mutations in other breast cancer susceptibility genes4 may explain a significant fraction of Spanish female breast cancer families.

We have found a significant excess of BRCA1 vs. BRCA2 mutations (3-fold) in Spanish families with no male breast cancer cases recorded. This excess of BRCA1 mutations is a common trait in many populations,25 although a similar prevalence for both genes28 or even an excess of BRCA2 mutations27 has been described for some populations.

It has been suggested that BRCA2 mutations are clustered in site-specific breast cancer families.4, 27, 29 This has been interpreted as evidence for an increase in ovarian cancer risk associated with BRCA1 but not BRCA2 mutations. Interestingly, 54% of Spanish BRCA2-related families identified in our study included cases of ovarian cancer. Some studies have postulated a BRCA2 central region, the ovarian cancer cluster region (OCCR),30, 31 which is a target of mutations associated with a higher risk of developing ovarian cancer. However, this genotype-phenotype correlation doesn't explain our results because in our study, 1 of 3 families with OCCR mutations and 6 of 10 families with non-OCCR mutations have at least 1 ovarian cancer case. Other investigators also failed to demonstrate such an association.24, 27 It is possible that some mutations outside the OCCR region are also associated with an increase in ovarian cancer risk. Alternatively, other genetic or environmental factors present in the Spanish population may increase the ovarian cancer risk associated with a BRCA2 mutation, but these associations require much larger sets of families to be discernible.

The data discussed above suggest that when male breast cancer cases are not present, BRCA1 must be the first gene to be tested in Spanish families, regardless of whether these are breast or breast/ovarian families.

Several studies suggest a correlation between male breast cancer and BRCA2 mutations,29 although the extent of this correlation is not clear. Linkage studies indicate that more than 80% of families with male breast cancer are related to BRCA2 mutations.4, 31 Other studies identified BRCA2 mutations in 50%24 or even only in 11% of these families.32 Moreover, one study did not find an association between male breast cancer and BRCA2 status in a Dutch population.33 We found BRCA2 mutations in all 5 families with male breast cancer, suggesting a strong association between these families and BRCA2 mutations in the Spanish population. Indeed, in our study, male breast cancer was the only cancer phenotype that clearly distinguished BRCA2 from BRCA1 families. We think that BRCA2 must be the first gene to be tested in Spanish families with male breast cancer.

We detected a statistically significant excess of families with prostate cancer cases in BRCA1/2-related families (4-fold). An increase of prostate incidence in both BRCA1 and BRCA2 carriers has been described, although the extent of this association has not been established definitively.7, 29 It is also interesting to note that we do not find an excess of cancer other than breast, ovarian and prostate in BRCA-positive families.

More than 500 different mutations have been described in BRCA1 and BRCA2 genes,10 but the number of different mutations in a defined population may be restricted. Specific populations for which recurrent mutations in BRCA1 and BRCA2 have been described include Scotland, The Netherlands and Belgium, Iceland, Finland, Russia, as well as French-Canadian and Ashkenazi populations.5, 27, 30, 33–35 Our study indicates that mutations 185delAG, 589delCT and Ala1708Glu may explain a significant fraction (63%) of BRCA1-related Spanish families. It may be recommendable to test the presence of these mutations as a first step of the screening protocol. Our study also suggests that most Spanish BRCA1-related families do not carry mutations located in exon 11. Although there is no obvious explanation for this observation, it may be efficient to analyse exon 11 in the final stage of the screening protocol.

Our analysis identified the number of ovarian cancer cases, the mean age at diagnosis of breast cancer, the presence of bilateral breast cancer, the presence of breast and ovarian cancer in a single woman and the presence of male breast cancer as major predictive factors for finding a mutation in a family. We have developed a logistic regression model that predicts the probability that a family carries a mutation in BRCA1 or BRCA2 as a function of these predictive factors. We think that this model may be a useful tool when deciding whether to refer a Spanish family to genetic testing or not. Like other studies, we have provided evidence for the predictive value of ovarian cancer and early onset of breast cancer,11, 12, 24 bilateral breast cancer11, 24 and breast and ovarian cancer in a single woman.11, 12 We did not find any predictive value for the number of unilateral breast cancer cases in the family. Others found either of low predictive value11 or of no predictive value at all.12 The latter study also failed to demonstrate a predictive value for the presence of bilateral breast cancer. In any case, a direct comparison of our model with previously published ones is difficult because these works were either restricted to BRCA111, 12 or analysed the cancer phenotype of the proband and not the family24 and, in general, had different clinical entry criteria.

Two recent studies present logistic regression models based on series of Finnish15 or Dutch33 families with 3 or more breast and/or ovarian cancer cases. The model by Vahteristo et al.15 includes the age of the youngest breast cancer patient and the number of ovarian cancers as the predictive variables and do not find a predictive value for female breast cancer, bilateral breast cancer or breast and ovarian cancer in a single woman. Male breast cancer is not considered. By contrast, the model by Peelen et al.33 includes the number of breast cancer patients, the mean age at diagnosis of breast cancer, the number of ovarian cancer patients and the presence of bilateral breast cancer as predictive variables. No predictive value is found for breast and ovarian cancer in a single woman or male breast cancer. Our predicting model includes the mean age at diagnosis of breast cancer, number of ovarian cancer patients and presence of bilateral breast cancer, breast and ovarian cancer in a single woman and male breast cancer as predictive variables. We do not find a predictive value for the number of female breast cancer cases. Thus, these 3 models show significant differences. Interestingly, both Finnish and Dutch populations exhibit different recurrent mutations in BRCA1 and BRCA215, 35 and our study indicates the presence of other recurrent BRCA1/2 mutations in the Spanish population. Possibly, different BRCA1 and BRCA2 mutation spectrums, together with different genetic and environmental factors, account for differences in the cancer phenotype of susceptible families, underscoring the relevance of predictive models specific for a given population.
